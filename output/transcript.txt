In recent years, machine learning has come a long way, introducing models that are more and more complex. From linear regression to SVM to trees, neuronet networks, and even large language models like chat GPT. But sometimes to truly understand how these models work, it is best to go back to the basics. Especially if you're a beginner, starting with simple yet powerful algorithms can make all the difference. So, let's take a step back and explore one of the most intuitive machine learning algorithms out there. Imagine you have a set of points, each labeled either blue or red. Now, I give you a new dot and ask you to classify it. Should it be blue or red? How would you decide? Most likely, you take a quick look at the nearby points. If most of the closest points are blue, you'd probably say the new dot should also be blue. And if they're red, you'd classify it as red instead. That instinctive approach, looking at the nearest neighbors and making a decision based on their labels, is exactly how K nearest neighbors or K&N works. First, we choose a value for K, which represents the number of neighbors to consider. Then we find the K nearest points to the new data point. We look at their labels. Are most of them red or blue? And finally, we assign the new point the most common label among its neighbors. That's it. No complicated math, no complex models, just simple intuitive decision-m. Now, to determine the nearest neighbors, we need a way to measure distances. The most common used metric is the ukidian distance, which calculates the straight line distance between two points. However, depending on the data, other metrics like the Manhattan distance or the Minowski distance can be more appropriate. Choosing the right distance metric can impact how well KN&N performs, especially in highdimensional spaces. KN&N is great for classification problems like we've seen, but it can also be used for regression. It's also a lazy learner, meaning it does not need to build a model beforehand. It just stores the data and makes predictions on demand. However, KN&N does have its limitations. It can be slow with large data sets and choosing the right K value is crucial for accuracy. Additionally, KN suffers from the curse of dimensionality. As the number of feature increases, the concept of distance becomes less meaningful making it hard for K&N to find relevant neighbors and this can significantly impact performance requiring techniques like dimensionality reduction to improve results. But as a starting point in machine learning, it's one of the best ways to understand how classification works in an intuitive way. So next time you need to classify something, just ask yourself, what do its nearest neighbors say? Because sometimes the simplest approach is the most effective.
